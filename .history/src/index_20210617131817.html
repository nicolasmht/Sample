Amen Break, “beat seller”!

The Amen break is a drum sample originally played by G.C. Coleman from The Winstons. 5 seconds without which the jungle and drum’n’bass would never have existed. Hip-hop wouldn't have the same face if the biggest producers hadn't been able to use it.

Jean Michel Jarre

In 1979, the introduction of the Fairlight CMI line of sampler synthesizers made it possible to edit and modify waveforms on the monitor. With such a machine, the musician can graphically manipulate the parameters of a sound. Jean Michel Jarre is one of the first users, his seventh album Zoolook is distinguished by the use of samples of 25 different ethnic voices as percussion. At the time, the price of a Fairlight was £ 25,000, which is equivalent to £ 25,000 today!

The SP 1 200, first numeric sampler

The SP-1 200 can store up to 100 patterns, 100 songs, and has a 5,000-note maximum memory for drum sequences. He considerably accentuated the use of Amen Break in musical compositions, by democratizing sampling among music composers.

The conflict, sampling’s limits

The limit of the sample stems from its ethical side, fueling many controversies. Its use is not regulated, disputes are legion. The group The Verve seems to be the perfect example! The track Bitter Sweet Symphony uses an (unauthorized) sample from The Last Time by The Rolling Stones. They were then forced to return 100% of their royalties, it was only recently that The Verve recovered the rights!
